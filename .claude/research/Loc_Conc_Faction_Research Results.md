# Location, Faction, and Concept Note Depth for AI-Assisted Interactive Fiction Worldbuilding

## TL;DR
- **The behavioral specification reframing transfers cleanly to all three note types.** A location note must answer "what HAPPENS here, who comes and why, and how does the place shape a scene"; a faction note must answer "how does the group ACT, such that any member reads as a member"; a concept note must answer "what does this constrain — what can and cannot happen because this is true." In every case the differentiator is the same shift the character blueprint made: from describing the entity to specifying its behavior.
- **Professional practice gives you ready-made section orders** — Nord Games' four-step settlement model (Basic Information → Community → Points of Interest → Extra Intrigue), World Anvil's four-tab Organization template, Stars Without Number's faction-as-agent model (attributes/assets/tags/goals), and Sanderson's limitation-first magic framework — but NONE of them were written for LLM scene-rendering, and the AI-RP community has essentially NO dedicated guidance for faction notes as behavioral specs. That gap is your opportunity.
- **The dominant failure mode across all three is the same:** underspecified notes cause the LLM to default to generic, plot-convenience output — flat "tavern is dimly lit" locations, interchangeable faction members who all sound alike, and concept notes too vague to ever constrain a scene. The fix is concrete, behavioral, instructional content, not more description.

## Key Findings

### The core reframing (answers Key Questions 1, 2, 4)

**Location as behavioral spec.** The character-blueprint question "what does this character DO in any situation?" has a direct location analog: **"what happens in this place, who comes here and why, and how does the place push back on a scene set in it?"** The strongest single articulation comes from fiction author Carmen Maria Machado, in *In the Dream House* (Graywolf, 2019), Ch. 43 "Dream House as World Building" (p. 72): *"Places are never just places in a piece of writing. If they are, the author has failed. Setting is not inert. It is activated by point of view."* The complementary "setting as character" test — *"If you can write a scene where the setting actively pushes back against the character, you are not writing a backdrop anymore. You are writing a character"* (AuthorFlows) — names the Overlook Hotel as the archetype: a location that functions as an antagonist. This is the location equivalent of behavioral specification — a place defined by what it *does* to the people in it, not how it looks.

**Faction as behavioral spec.** A faction note can absolutely be a behavioral specification, and the model already exists in TTRPG practice even though the AI-RP community has not formalized it. Vampire: The Masquerade's clan system is the clearest example: each clan is written as *a set of behavioral tendencies and social roles*, not a roster. The official Paradox Interactive clan descriptions are almost entirely behavioral — Ventrue "members are expected to assume command wherever possible, and they're willing to endure storms for the sake of leading from the front"; Lasombra "prefer to act as puppeteers, powers behind the proverbial throne"; Nosferatu "lurk on the fringes of most cities, acting as spies and brokers of information." Crucially, VtM separates **Nature** (true self) from **Demeanor** (the mask presented outward) and treats stereotypes as "a combination of what the average member of a clan is like and what other clans think they act like" (Onyx Path forums) — giving you a built-in mechanism for *members who share identity without being interchangeable*.

**Concept as constraint, not description.** The narrative purpose of a concept note — beyond keyword mechanics — is to **constrain what can happen in a scene**. The governing framework is Sanderson's Second Law, stated verbatim on his own site as *"Limitations > Powers,"* with the elaboration that *"the best limitations will have real effects on the characters, rather than pretend ones... no real emotional or economic cost... Look for things that tie the magic to other setting elements and which make life hard for the practitioners."* A concept note that lists what a magic system/institution/practice CAN do produces generic output; one that specifies what it CANNOT do, what it costs, and what it's vulnerable to actually constrains the scene. The question a good concept note answers that a bad one doesn't: **"what does the existence of this thing make impossible, costly, or inevitable?"**

### Section structures from professional practice (answers Key Question 3)

**Location — the most validated structure** is Nord Games' *Spectacular Settlements* (a 482-page sourcebook by Andrew Geertsen; its Kickstarter raised over US$100,000 from 2,700+ backers) and its four-step model, used across thousands of published settlements: **(1) Basic Information** (broad/fundamental details — origin, size, environment, condition), **(2) Community** (the people who live here and aspects of society), **(3) Points of Interest** (shops, services, places of worship — the places things happen), **(4) Extra Intrigue** (optional flavor/secrets). World Anvil's Settlement template corroborates and extends this with prompts for Demographics, Guilds & Factions, History, Industry & Trade, Defences, Architecture, and Tourism. The SillyTavern community's "Environment" PList format is the LLM-native compression of this: `[Mossford: town, mossy buildings, has(tavern, bank, inn, castle), kind people, wealthy]`.

**Faction — the most validated structure** is World Anvil's four-tab Organization template: **Generic** (founding, leader, history, assets, structure, culture), **Geopolitical** (for nation-scale entities), plus relationship/rank fields. Stars Without Number provides the *behavioral-agent* model: every faction is a goal-seeking actor with **Attributes** (Force/Cunning/Wealth), **Assets**, **Tags** (descriptive modifiers shaping what they're good at and what they want), and **Goals** that advance turn-by-turn. Eberron's dragonmarked houses show the membership-and-monopoly model (what the group controls, its internal hierarchy, its tenets/laws).

### Three-part structural analogs to Foundation/Behavior/Relationships (answers Key Question 4a)
No professional framework uses the exact "Body/Environment/Soul" triad for locations or factions, so these are **synthesized inferences** grounded in the sources:
- **Location:** Physical form (Nord's "Basic Information") → Social/economic life (Nord's "Community" + Points of Interest) → History & meaning (Nord's "Extra Intrigue" + World Anvil's History). This maps cleanly to Body/Environment/Soul.
- **Faction:** Origin & purpose (SWN Goals + WA founding/history) → Collective behavior (VtM clan-style behavioral tendencies — the missing piece in most templates) → Inter-faction web (Blades relationship map). This maps to Foundation/Behavioral Descriptions/Relationships.

## Details

### 1a. Location documentation in professional worldbuilding
- **TTRPG sourcebooks (documented practice).** Nord Games' *Spectacular Settlements* (482 pp.) is the most explicit published structure: four steps (Basic Information, Community, Points of Interest, Extra Intrigue), differentiated by scale — Trading Posts → Villages → Towns → Cities → Capitals → Fortresses, each chapter with its own step breakdown. This answers the region/settlement/building question: the **same skeleton scales**, but larger settlements get more sub-sections (cities and capitals get more Points of Interest and political detail). Pathfinder's settlement statblock (d20PFSRD) is the opposite extreme — mechanical attributes (Government, Qualities, Danger, Disadvantages, marketplace values) rather than narrative. The contrast is instructive: **Pathfinder documents a location as a ruleset; Nord documents it as a community.** For LLM scene-rendering, the Nord/community model is far more useful.
- **Narrative-game documentation (documented practice, thin sourcing).** Game writers' published accounts (Brooke Maggs on *The Gardens Between*; the Ross Berger narrative-design text) establish that in large story games like Dragon Age there are *teams* of writers working from "story documents, flow charts and regular meetings," and that narrative designers "have input in designing... the environment, the world, the gameplay" and "write briefs for the other writers." But specific *location-documentation templates* for writers are not publicly published — this is a genuine gap in available sourcing. The transferable principle: locations are documented for writers in terms of what story function they serve, not their geography.
- **Novelists/screenwriters (documented practice).** The TV "show bible" is the closest published analog. Gangs of London's bible is explicitly cited as defining "its setting through power structures... London is both a backdrop and an arena shaped by competing forces" (Celtx). The dominant professional warning: "Detailed world-building only works if it affects behaviour and conflict. Explaining rules or lore without showing why they matter can quickly bog the document down. Include world details that actively shape story and leave the rest out" (Celtx). This is the location equivalent of behavioral specification stated by working TV writers.

### 1b. What a location note needs for an LLM
- **Documented failure mode (community + inference).** The MegaNova creator guide describes the canonical decay pattern: a character "feels flat" by message 10 and "they don't reference their environment at all" by message 20. The SillyTavern community's "spatial context map" feature request (GitHub #4731) documents the underlying problem explicitly: without spatial/relational data "the model [can't] actually reason... spatially," producing locations that are inert backdrops. When a location note is underspecified, the LLM defaults to **stock sensory clichés** ("dimly lit, smoky tavern") and **plot-convenience geography** (rooms and exits appear as needed, with no consistency).
- **What "works" vs. what doesn't (community-established).** The World Info Encyclopedia (kingbri/Alicat/Trappu — the canonical ST reference) prescribes the "Environment" PList: a compressed attribute list keyed to triggers, placed *before* the character description "since it's never highlighted in the chat itself, but the character is aware of it." The repeated community warning: "Don't Overstuff Your Entries... give the AI just enough information to be creative, not to write the story for it" (Arsturn), and "Use Adjectives & Sensory Details... 'The sword is magical' is boring." The differentiator between an entry that works and one that doesn't is **evocative specificity at low token cost** — concrete sensory and behavioral hooks, not exhaustive description.
- **The key location question (synthesized).** Combining Machado ("setting is activated by point of view"), the "setting as character" test, and the fiction-craft consensus that a place should "feel differently at different times": the question that separates vivid from generic is **"who comes here, what do they want, and what does this place do to a scene that no other place would?"**

### 1c. Location-as-system and behavioral register
- **Living-location frameworks (documented).** World Anvil's Settlement template explicitly prompts for "Guilds and Factions... These factions shape the policy and economy of the settlement" — i.e., the location is documented as a social ecosystem of competing interests. Nord's "Community" step does the same. This is the "who comes here, what do they want, what conflicts exist" model.
- **Behavioral register / a place at different times (documented craft practice).** Fiction-craft sources are unanimous that a place must be rendered differently by time, weather, and observer. Lucy Cruickshanks' worked example (Writers & Artists): two characters "visit the same locations, but they feel very differently about them. They notice different details and use different words." Reedsy's rule of thumb: "each time your characters... experience that location in a new context (e.g. at night rather than in the daytime) — you should devote a paragraph to setting the scene." The transferable framework for a location note: **specify the register shifts** — how the place reads at day vs. night, to an insider vs. outsider, in calm vs. crisis.

### 2a. Faction documentation in professional worldbuilding
- **VtM (documented, strongest behavioral model).** Clans are defined by psychological tendencies, social roles, and a clan weakness — "factions defined not by biology... but by psychological tendencies, cultural identities, and social roles. Each clan carries a distinct personality profile" (Simply Put Psych analysis). The **Nature/Demeanor** split and the explicit treatment of stereotypes-vs-individuals is the key transferable mechanism for collective identity without interchangeability.
- **Stars Without Number (documented, strongest agent model).** Factions are mechanical agents: Attributes (Force/Cunning/Wealth), Hit Points, Assets, Tags, and Goals that earn XP and advance each turn. "The idea is to let the events of faction turn mold the world" — factions act on the world whether or not the protagonist is present.
- **Eberron dragonmarked houses (documented, membership/monopoly model).** Documented via: what each house controls (monopoly/guilds), internal hierarchy (matriarch/patriarch + retainers), the tenets all houses follow (Korth Edicts, neutrality), and member relationship-to-house options (Agent, Independent Scion, Excoriate, Foundling) — a built-in framework for **membership ambiguity**.
- **TV bibles (documented).** Gangs of London defines factions as the "engine that generates conflict"; characters are written by "function as much as personality... Each major figure destabilises the system in a specific way."
- **Internal dynamics (documented).** World Anvil's Organization template explicitly supports factions-within-factions ("many organizations can have organizations within them"), and its sect prompt asks "What factions and sects exist within... How do they differ from the main dogma?... Why do they differ?"

### 2b. Faction as behavioral entity — and the AI-RP gap
- **The major finding (community, via dedicated sub-research): there is essentially NO dedicated AI-RP guidance for writing faction/group entries as behavioral specs.** Every major lorebook guide treats factions as *descriptive* lore. The canonical World Info Encyclopedia splits entries only into "Environment" and "Lore" and states outright "This is NOT a guide on how to write characters" — behavioral content is handled per-character via Ali:Chat reaction examples, never as a faction-wide behavior block. MegaNova's "5 Lorebook Entry Types" subordinates factions to descriptive "Lore" (its example "Mages Guild" entry is pure hierarchy/laws/politics with no member behavior) and reserves "Behavioral Rules" entries for individual named characters. AI Dungeon's Faction category advises keeping faction info "generic." NovelAI lists factions as "goals, leadership, members, conflicts" only. **This is the single biggest opportunity in the skill set: nobody has packaged "faction = shared behavioral spec."**
- **What the LLM needs to make members read as members (synthesized from transferable techniques).** Three documented techniques can be assembled into a faction-behavior spec, though no source assembles them for factions: (1) **Instructional, behavior-focused phrasing** beats descriptive — MegaNova: *BAD* "Kaelen touches his scar when nervous"; *GOOD* "[When nervous, Kaelen touches his scar unconsciously. Show this in responses.]" Applied to factions: "Members of the Sash always address outsiders with measured suspicion; show this," not "the Sash are suspicious." (2) **Concrete behavioral keywords beat abstract/evaluative ones** (AI Dungeon WI research: "Broad words like good or evil aren't very useful, when the behavior is what we are after"). (3) **sphiratrioth666's "procedural guided generation"** (Hugging Face) — inserting lorebook entries as System-role behavioral *instructions* rather than lore — "influencing the actual {{char}} behavior... seems to work better than pure card definitions."
- **Distinct-but-unified members (synthesized).** The technique is "shared values/mannerisms + individual variation layer": a shared faction entry plus per-member override entries (mechanically enabled by SillyTavern's Character Filters). Robo Rhythms: "Give each character a distinct voice — think quirks, slang... One of my bots always responds with overly formal language. Another? Total sarcasm gremlin." VtM's Nature/Demeanor split is the professional version: shared clan Demeanor norms, individual Nature underneath.

### 2c. Faction relationships and change
- **Inter-faction documentation (documented).** Blades in the Dark's faction system is the gold standard: factions have a **Tier** (power), **Status** (-3 to +3 relationship to the PCs/each other), and **clocks** tracking goals. The relationship web is deliberately lightweight — "you look at their direct relationship (who they are allied with, who they are fighting, what do they want). No giant relationship web required" (Stras Acimovic, BitD designer). The "tug-of-war clock" models back-and-forth conflict between two factions.
- **Faction change over time (documented).** The Blades answer to "how does faction state change without constant rewriting" is the **clock**: you don't rewrite the faction, you advance a segmented progress clock toward its goal. Critically, the community consensus is to only track clocks for factions "that are doing things that I think are interesting... stuff that bears directly on the PCs" (RPGnet) — faction state for off-screen groups is resolved with a "fortune roll" when needed, not pre-simulated. This is a strong anti-over-documentation principle.
- **Interactive-fiction reputation systems (documented).** SWN's faction turn is explicitly designed so "those radio transmissions and other rumours your PCs hear are actually based on what happened" — the documentation layer for a reputation system is goals + assets + a turn procedure, not static prose.

### 3a. What makes a concept entry work
- **"Behavioral rules" entries are the highest-impact type (community).** The MegaNova guide's strongest claim, attributed to a "Top SillyTavern Creator": *"I spend 60% of my time on behavioral rules. That's what makes a character feel ALIVE. Appearance is forgettable, behavior is memorable."* For world rules/cultural practices/event history, the same instructional framing applies: write the rule as a directive the model should *enact*, not a fact it should *know*.
- **Framing and what to exclude (community).** Consensus across ST guides: one concept per entry; concise; evocative over exhaustive; don't duplicate across character card and lorebook ("Choose ONE source of truth... Never duplicate critical info!" — MegaNova). The World Info Encyclopedia warns against over-importance: "As a writer, you may think that everything is important to your character. This is not the case and should be avoided to prevent possible leakage."

### 3b. Concept note depth in worldbuilding frameworks
- **Surface vs. deep documentation (documented).** Sanderson's framework is the spine: hard vs. soft magic (First Law — only resolve conflict with magic the audience understands), limitations over powers (Second Law), and "expand what you already have before you add something new" (Third Law — depth over breadth). This directly validates the settled three-layer surface/mid/deep concept structure.
- **What deserves its own note (documented inference).** Sanderson's Third Law ("expand before you add") is the decision rule: a concept earns its own note when it can be *expanded with depth and connected to existing elements*, not when it merely adds novelty. World Anvil's template-routing guidance gives a practical taxonomy (magic system → Natural Law; organized religion → Organization; belief system → Tradition).
- **The iceberg model — "what everyone knows" vs. "what is true" (documented).** Hemingway's Iceberg Theory ("theory of omission") is the canonical frame, stated in *Death in the Afternoon* (1932), Ch. 16: *"The dignity of movement of an ice-berg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water."* The worldbuilding gloss is Jenna Blum's (in *The Author at Work*, 2013): the writer's hidden knowledge "acts as the bulk of the iceberg. And that is what gives your story weight and gravitas." The Obsidian Tavern's worldbuilding application names the failure mode precisely: surface-level worldbuilding is when "characters know exactly what they need to know for each scene but never display knowledge outside their immediate purpose," and the fix is the "casual mention" technique — characters reference "the guild troubles in Westport" without explanation. For a concept note, this means documenting **both layers explicitly**: the public/believed version AND the true version, so the LLM can have characters act on belief while the truth constrains outcomes.

### 3c. Concept notes as world constraints
- **Constraint vs. description (documented).** Sanderson again: a concept is documented *enough to constrain a scene* when it specifies limitations, costs, and weaknesses — "the best limitations will have real effects on the characters... economic cost... Look for things that tie the magic to other setting elements and which make life hard for the practitioners." A magic system documented only by its powers cannot constrain; one documented by its costs and limits can.
- **Hard rules vs. soft flavor (documented).** The First Law is the dividing line: a rule worth documenting explicitly as a hard constraint is one the story will use to *resolve or block* conflict (it must be understood to be fair); soft flavor is what preserves mystery and should NOT be used to resolve plot. A rule is worth making explicit when contradicting it would break the audience's trust.

### 4b. Completion criteria / over-documentation
- **Documented "done" heuristics.** Multiple working writers converge: a setting's importance "is directly proportional to how much of an impact that setting has upon the characters" (Cameron Montague Taylor) — document only what touches a scene. A concrete over-documentation test: if your background notes reach novella length (one author cites 26,000+ words of notes), you've crossed into procrastination (Joey D'Urso). The TTRPG version: "the only worldbuilding you really need is the stuff that facilitates a reasonable amount of verisimilitude... or that sparks player interest" (D&D Beyond forums). **The "done" criterion: a note is complete when it can render the behavior/scenes it will actually be called on for — and over-documented when it adds detail no scene will ever touch.**

### 4c. Underspecification failure modes (answers Key Question 6)
- **Locations:** flat, directionless scenes; stock sensory clichés; inconsistent/plot-convenience geography; characters that "don't reference their environment at all" (MegaNova); no spatial reasoning (ST GitHub #4731).
- **Factions:** the documented killer. SillyTavern's *own official docs* warn that combining group members into "one big blob of text... can lead to... characters being confused about themselves, having merged personalities, uncertain traits." Community reports: members "all sound the same," voices "blend together," one member dominates while others "vanish into the void" (Robo Rhythms); personality/allegiance drift over long chats. When faction identity is absent, the LLM defaults to making every member a generic instance of their surface role (every guard gruff, every merchant greedy) or bleeds them toward the {{user}}'s register.
- **Concepts — too sparse vs. too dense:** too sparse = the concept never constrains anything (a "magic system" that's just "magic exists" lets the model invent anything, breaking consistency); too dense = token bloat that causes the model to ignore or lose instructions. The LLM-context research is unanimous that irrelevant/excessive context "can confuse the model" and degrade focus, and that concise instructions "often achieve comparable results with far fewer tokens." The sweet spot: enough concrete limitation to constrain, no inert lore.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Adopt these section orders now (they're validated).**
- **Location note:** (1) *Physical form* — what it is, scale, condition, environment; (2) *Social/economic life* — who lives/works here, who comes and why, what they want, what conflicts exist (the "Community" + factions layer); (3) *Behavioral register* — how the place reads at different times/to different people/in crisis; (4) *History & meaning* — why it exists, what it implies, secrets. Map this to Body/Environment/Soul.
- **Faction note:** (1) *Origin & purpose* — founding, what it controls, its goal(s); (2) *Collective behavior* — the VtM-clan layer, written as instructional directives ("members do X in situation Y because Z"), including the Nature/Demeanor split (how members present vs. who they are); (3) *Inter-faction web* — direct allies/rivals/contested relationships only (Blades-style, no giant web); (4) *Membership* — ranks, how one joins/leaves, and ambiguity cases (former members, dual loyalty). Map this to Foundation/Behavioral Descriptions/Relationships.

**Stage 2 — Build the faction-behavior layer that nobody else has.** This is your highest-value differentiator. Write the "Collective behavior" section as a **When/Behavior/Because table mirroring the character blueprint**: trigger situation → how a member acts → the faction value that drives it. Include a **distinctness mechanism** (shared Demeanor norms + a prompt to vary individual Nature) so members read as members without becoming clones. Phrase everything as instructional directives, not description.

**Stage 3 — Make concept notes constrain, not describe.** For every concept note, require an explicit **limitations/costs/weaknesses** block (Sanderson's Second Law) and, where relevant, a **two-layer iceberg split** (what everyone believes vs. what is true). A concept note without a limitations block is description, not a constraint, and should be flagged incomplete.

**Stage 4 — Bake in completion/anti-bloat self-checks.** Add a "done" test to each note type: *Can this note render the scenes/behavior it will actually be called on for?* And an over-documentation test: *Does any section add detail no scene will touch, or duplicate the character card?* If yes, cut it. Track faction state via lightweight "clock"-style progress markers rather than rewriting notes, and only for factions that bear on active scenes.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** If you observe LLM output where faction members are already distinct without a behavior layer, downgrade Stage 2. If concept notes with limitations blocks still produce unconstrained scenes, the problem is keyword/trigger mechanics (settled elsewhere), not content. If token budgets are tight, prioritize the behavioral/instructional content and cut history/flavor first — the research consistently shows behavior is what survives contact with a long chat.

## Caveats
- **The strongest faction and concept frameworks come from TTRPGs and fiction craft, not AI-RP.** They were designed for human GMs and readers, not LLM context windows. The behavioral-spec *reframing* is well-supported, but the specific LLM-rendering application is largely synthesized inference, not documented practice — flagged as such throughout.
- **The AI-RP faction gap is real and confirmed by dedicated sub-research.** No authoritative community source packages factions as behavioral specs; the richest behavioral how-to material comes from vendor blogs (MegaNova, Arsturn) and individual creators (sphiratrioth666) rather than official docs or peer consensus. Treat specific numeric settings (priorities, probabilities) as opinion.
- **"Setting as character" and the iceberg model are craft heuristics, not empirical findings.** They're widely taught and internally consistent, but their effectiveness for LLM rendering specifically is inferred. (The "setting as character" test is attributed to the AuthorFlows craft guide; no more authoritative primary source was located for that specific framing.)
- **Narrative-game location-documentation templates for writers are not publicly available.** Claims about Dragon Age/Witcher/Mass Effect pipelines rest on general developer commentary, not leaked or published location GDDs. This is a genuine sourcing limitation.
- **Some sources are vendor/marketing content** (Arsturn, MegaNova, Sider, Jenova) with unverified attributed quotes; corroborated where possible against primary sources (official SillyTavern docs, the World Info Encyclopedia, Sanderson's own site, Blades in the Dark's own rules, Paradox's official VtM clan pages, and Machado's and Hemingway's published texts).
