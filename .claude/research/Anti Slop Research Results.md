# Positive Style Model for AI Specification Writing: Recommendation and Survey

## TL;DR
- **Use the screenwriting "action line" / scene-description convention as the primary named model**, invoked as *"Write each behavior the way a screenplay writes action lines — present tense, only what can be seen or heard, no internal states"*; it is the only high-salience reference that natively solves your hardest failure mode (vague interiority), because the form structurally forbids unobservable interior description, and the spec→enactment relationship is identical to the script→performance relationship.
- **The "write like X" approach will not work on its own.** Peer-reviewed evidence (Reinhart et al., *PNAS* 2025) shows the noun-heavy, Latinate, informationally dense register is an instruction-tuning artifact that persists *even when the model is explicitly told to match another genre's style, tone, and diction* — so a named model must be paired with 2–3 before/after few-shot exemplars drawn from your own failure cases, which is the most reliable lever on surface form.
- **Reject Hemingway as primary** (his iceberg/withholding ethos is wrong for specs and "write like Hemingway" reliably produces atmospheric melodrama); **demote Orwell to a secondary vocabulary anchor** (excellent on Latinate/copula/inflation, hugely salient, but silent on behavioral concreteness and arguably too generic).

## Key Findings

1. **"AI slop" is now a formally studied quality problem, and its diagnosed symptoms match your failure list.** Shaib, Chakrabarty, Garcia-Olano & Wallace (Northeastern University, Stony Brook University, Meta AI), "Measuring AI 'Slop' in Text," arXiv:2509.19163 (24 Sept 2025), "develop[s] a taxonomy of 'slop' through interviews with experts in NLP, writing, and philosophy," grouping it into Information Utility, Information Quality, and Style Quality, with style codes including Verbosity, Word Complexity, Vagueness/Density, and Tone. A PNAS study quantifies the syntactic signature: GPT-4o uses present participial clauses at 5.3× the human rate, nominalizations 2.1×, "that"-clause subjects 2.6×, and phrasal coordination 1.9×. These are precisely your copula-avoidance and significance-inflation patterns expressed as grammar.

2. **The vocabulary is content-activated, not just register-activated.** GPT-4o uses words like *camaraderie, palpable, tapestry,* and *intricate* at more than 100× the human rate; "tapestry" appears in 23% of outputs and "amidst" in 27%. The PNAS authors note these are unremarkable in fiction but conspicuous across all genres — confirming your hypothesis that Latinate reaches are triggered by the writing task itself, not by a register the prompt can simply re-set. The phenomenon is large-scale and measurable: Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát & Lause, "Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary," *Science Advances* 11(27):eadt3813 (2 July 2025), analyzed >15M PubMed abstracts (2010–2024) and found "at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts were processed with LLMs … reaching 40% for some subcorpora," driven by exactly these style-affecting verbs and adjectives.

3. **The decisive empirical result: style-imitation prompting does not suppress the dense default.** Reinhart, Markey, Laudenbach, Pantusen, Yurko, Weinberg & Brown, *PNAS* 122(8):e2422455122 (Feb 2025) prompted models with a human writing sample and instructed them to continue "in the same style, tone, and diction" across six genres including fiction and TV/movie scripts. The density persisted. Their Significance statement: *"instruction-tuned models … have a distinct noun-heavy, informationally dense writing style, even when prompted to match the style of informal speech and writing,"* and instruction tuning "limits their ability to mimic other writing styles." This is the core caveat your brief asked about: persona/named-style instructions alone are a weak suppression mechanism for content-driven patterns.

4. **Exemplars and structural constraints outperform pure instruction for surface form.** Practitioner and academic consensus is that few-shot examples are the strongest lever on tone/structure/format. A controlled 2025 study (Bohr, "Show and Tell," arXiv:2511.13972) found, for verbosity control, that concise *instructions* gave the most durable control while *examples* shaped initial form but let "the model's default verbosity … partially persist" — meaning neither alone is sufficient and both should be combined. (Caveat: this study is on code generation with Gemini 2.5 Pro, not prose.)

5. **The screenwriting action-line convention is the cleanest structural match for behavioral concreteness.** Action lines are written in present tense, describe only what the audience can see or hear, and explicitly *avoid describing internal thoughts or emotions* — the rule set converts "she is anxious" into "she paces, checks the door." This is exactly the translation your "vague interiority" failure mode needs, and it is the only candidate where behavioral concreteness is the defining constraint rather than an add-on.

## Details

### Survey findings (Phase 1)
The discourse on AI writing quality has matured from blog complaints into measurement. The leading academic effort, Shaib et al.'s "Measuring AI 'Slop' in Text" (arXiv:2509.19163, 2025), built a taxonomy from interviews with 19 experts in writing, journalism, linguistics, NLP, and philosophy, and found that even capable reasoning LLMs fail to reliably detect their own slop — which is why rule-based self-policing (your blacklist approach) underperforms. The taxonomy's Style Quality axis (Verbosity, Word Complexity, Vagueness, Tone, Repetition) maps directly onto your failure modes. Parallel work — Reinhart et al. (PNAS 2025) on grammatical style, Kobak et al. (*Science Advances* 2025) on excess vocabulary in PubMed abstracts, and a writing-quality reward-model line (Chakrabarty et al.) — converges on the same picture: instruction tuning produces a noun-heavy, nominalized, Latinate-leaning register that is stable across prompts.

Proposed solutions cluster into four families. (a) **Detection/measurement** (slop taxonomies, DetectGPT/Binoculars, excess-word lists) — useful for evaluation, not generation. (b) **Fine-tuning / reward modeling** (Writing Quality Reward Models; the LAMP corpus of human-polished AI text) — effective but heavyweight. (c) **Prompt engineering** — persona prompting, style guides, and few-shot exemplars. (d) **Editorial guidelines** — the plain-language movement and house style guides. The most important Phase-1 finding for your decision is in family (c): the literature is explicitly mixed-to-negative on persona prompting. Zheng, Pei, Logeswaran, Lee & Jurgens, "When 'A Helpful Assistant' Is Not Really Helpful," Findings of EMNLP 2024, tested 162 roles across 4 LLM families and 2,410 factual questions and "demonstrate that adding personas in system prompts does not improve model performance" and "might actually hurt" it, with best-persona selection "no better than random selection"; relatedly, Hu & Collier (cited therein) found personas "often explain less than 10% of the variance in annotations on subjective NLP datasets." Crucially, the PNAS continuation experiment shows the dense register survives direct genre-imitation instructions. **The honest conclusion: a "write like X" instruction will shift tone and cadence but will not, by itself, reliably suppress content-activated Latinate vocabulary and nominalized density. It must be combined with concrete exemplars and structural constraints.** This does not make Phase 2 unnecessary — a named model is still the most efficient way to set register and to carry the behavioral-concreteness requirement — but it reframes the deliverable: the named model is the spine, not the whole skeleton.

### Primary recommendation: the screenwriting action-line / scene-description convention

**Why it is well-represented in training data.** Screenplays and screenwriting instruction are abundant on the open web and in book corpora: thousands of produced scripts, screenwriting craft sites (No Film School, Backstage, Celtx, Script Magazine), and the universal "show, don't tell" maxim. A model has seen enormous volumes of action-line prose and explicit instruction about how to write it, so "write like a screenplay's action lines" resolves to a consistent, recognizable target.

**Which failure modes it addresses, and how.**
- *Vague interiority (your primary failure mode):* Action-line craft's defining rule is to describe only what is visible/audible and to **avoid internal thoughts or emotions** — characters' states are shown through movement, body language, and reaction. This forces "She carries grief she has never articulated" → "She changes the subject and starts wiping down the counter." No other candidate makes this conversion the core of its identity.
- *Copula avoidance / significance inflation:* Action lines favor strong present-tense action verbs over linking constructions and contain no editorial "this underscores/reflects" commentary; the convention has no slot for significance announcements.
- *Literary flair:* The tradition explicitly warns against atmospheric overwriting and "purple prose," prizing brevity (commonly one-to-four lines per block) and "if it sounds like writing, rewrite it."
- *Latinate vocabulary:* Partly addressed (action verbs skew concrete/Anglo-Saxon), but this is the dimension where the named model is weakest and where exemplars must do the work — consistent with the PNAS finding.

**Why it fits the use case structurally.** Your specs are enacted by an AI game master in a scene. That is precisely what an action line is: a written instruction that a director and actors *enact*. The medium's entire reason for existing — "movies are about the external; have the character DO something" — is your spec requirement. The match is not analogical; it is functional.

**What the instruction looks like in practice.**
> "Write each behavioral spec the way a screenplay writes action lines. Present tense. Describe only what can be seen or heard in the scene. Never state an internal feeling or significance — show it through a concrete action the game master can stage. Short declarative sentences. Plain words."

Then attach 2–3 of your own worst before/after rewrites as few-shot exemplars (e.g., "BEFORE: Her silence stretches like frost across the room. AFTER: She goes quiet when this comes up and won't re-engage until the subject passes."). The exemplars carry the vocabulary suppression the named model can't guarantee alone.

### Close alternatives

**Elmore Leonard (named author; strong second).** Leonard's "10 Rules of Writing" are explicit, famous, and in training data, and his stated goal is to "show rather than tell": characters revealed through action and dialogue, "never use a verb other than 'said,'" no adverbs, "avoid detailed descriptions," "if it sounds like writing, rewrite it." This squarely hits flair, copula, and significance inflation, and partially hits interiority. Two liabilities keep him below the primary: (1) his ethos of authorial "invisibility" and "leave out the parts readers skip" tilts toward *omission*, which risks dropping spec detail you need (the same problem as Hemingway's iceberg); (2) he is a fiction stylist, so some narrative flavor leaks in. Strong as a co-anchor — "follow Elmore Leonard's rules: show through action, use 'said,' cut anything that sounds like writing" — but the screenwriting convention is safer because it demands *complete* observable description rather than artful withholding.

**George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (best vocabulary anchor, not primary).** Orwell's six rules (no stale metaphor; never a long word where a short one will do; cut every cuttable word; active over passive; no jargon/foreign phrase with an everyday equivalent) are the most precise public articulation of your *vocabulary* failure modes, and the essay's salience is enormous and citable. But evaluated against your three questions, it falls short as primary on one axis the brief flags as decisive: it addresses plain prose, not **behavioral concreteness** — it tells the model to avoid Latinate words and dead metaphors, but says nothing about converting interior states into stageable action. Its ubiquity is double-edged: it is so universally invoked as generic "good writing" advice that "follow Orwell's rules" may pull toward generic plainness rather than your specific spec register. Best use: a secondary instruction — "and apply Orwell's rules from 'Politics and the English Language': short Anglo-Saxon words, active voice, cut every unnecessary word."

**Hemingway (reject as primary).** He is the most salient plain-prose author and his Anglo-Saxon, short-sentence, subject-verb-object style matches much of your target. But two disqualifiers: (1) his defining principle is the **iceberg theory** — deliberately omitting seven-eighths of meaning — which your brief explicitly rules out for specification writing; (2) empirically, "write like Hemingway" prompts produce atmospheric, melodramatic output. This is documented in Word Savvy's "Tone? Why Hemingway's style works for cyber security" (wordsavvy.co.uk), where a ChatGPT "write like Hemingway" prompt rendered a breach as "The breach came quietly, like the fall of night" and "They moved through the networks like shadows" — i.e., it *adds* the literary flair you are trying to remove. Salience is high, but style-match and instruction-effect both fail.

**Behavioral-science operational definitions / ABA topographic definitions (best pure match, low salience).** Operational definitions are the gold standard for exactly your interiority problem: they forbid mentalistic terms ("angry," "frustrated") and require observable, countable description — "Student refuses to follow directions by saying 'no,' turning away, or remaining seated." Style-match is arguably the best of any candidate. The problem is salience and invocation reliability: "write like an ABA operational definition" or "like DSM diagnostic criteria" is not a register the model can reproduce as consistently as screenplay action lines, and the clinical tone is colder than a game spec wants. Worth borrowing as a *technique* ("define each trait the way a behavior analyst writes an operational definition — observable, no mental-state words") but weaker as the headline reference.

**Federal Plain Language Guidelines / plain-language movement (citable, narrow).** The guidelines (use concrete everyday words, active voice, present tense, strong verbs, short sentences, "use 'must'") are an authoritative, named, citable standard that nails vocabulary, copula, and inflation. But they are a bureaucratic-clarity standard with no doctrine on behavioral concreteness or scene enactment, and "plain language" is closer to the "generic plain writing advice" the brief warns is insufficient. Useful as a citable backstop, not the spine.

## Recommendations

1. **Adopt a layered instruction with the screenwriting action-line convention as the spine.** Lead with: *"Write each behavioral spec the way a screenplay writes action lines — present tense, only what can be seen or heard, no internal states or significance, short plain sentences."* This single reference carries your hardest requirement (behavioral concreteness / anti-interiority) and most of the flair/copula/inflation suppression.

2. **Attach 2–3 before/after few-shot exemplars from your real failure log.** This is the empirically strongest lever (PNAS shows instruction alone won't kill content-activated density) and the only reliable suppressor of Latinate vocabulary. Put the strongest exemplar last.

3. **Add Orwell as a one-line vocabulary co-anchor:** *"Apply Orwell's rules: shortest Anglo-Saxon word that works, active voice, cut every word that can go."* Use Elmore Leonard interchangeably if you want a "show-through-action" flavor, but keep your own "state everything needed" rule to neutralize his withholding bias.

4. **Keep one structural guardrail the named model can't supply:** a maximum sentence length and a ban on the specific nominalization frames PNAS flags (present participial openers, "X serves as," "stands as a testament to"). Named style sets register; a hard structural rule catches the residue.

5. **Benchmarks that would change the recommendation.** Measure outputs on (a) rate of mental-state words with no adjacent observable action, (b) copula-replacement frequency ("serves as/functions as/stands as"), and (c) Latinate/nominalization density. *Thresholds:* if the action-line spine alone cuts interiority violations by less than ~50% in A/B testing, the problem is invocation reliability — move budget from instruction to exemplars (add more, more diverse). If vocabulary (C) stays high despite exemplars, the residue is content-activated and only fine-tuning or a post-generation rewrite pass will close it. If significance-inflation (the "underscores/testament to" family) persists, it is structural and belongs in a hard banned-frame rule, not the style model.

## Caveats
- **The single biggest caveat is empirical and load-bearing:** Reinhart et al. (PNAS 2025) demonstrate the dense, noun-heavy register survives explicit genre-imitation prompts. Treat the named model as necessary but not sufficient; do not expect "write like action lines" alone to fix vocabulary.
- **Persona-prompting evidence is mixed and prompt-sensitive.** Zheng et al. (EMNLP Findings 2024) found 162 personas gave no gain on objective factual QA and selection was "no better than random"; effects are also unstable across exact wording — so test the precise phrasing and lock it once it works.
- **"Show and Tell" (the instructions-beat-exemplars result) is from code generation, not prose**, so its specific ranking may not transfer; the safe reading is that instructions + exemplars together beat either alone.
- **Several supporting sources are practitioner blogs and craft sites** (screenwriting guidance, the Word Savvy Hemingway-prompt experiment); they are reliable for describing conventions and illustrative behavior but are not controlled studies. The controlled claims rest on the PNAS and arXiv papers, the Kobak et al. *Science Advances* study, and the Shaib et al. taxonomy.
- **All three named candidates are fiction/clarity traditions, not spec traditions**, so each leaks something (Leonard/Hemingway: narrative flavor and withholding; Orwell/plain-language: no behavioral doctrine). The layered approach is designed to cancel each one's specific leak.
