# Intimate Dynamics (Optional Section)

*Sub-file for `worldbuilder-character-blueprint`. Only read this if the character's roster entry is flagged `Intimate Dynamics: Yes`. If the flag is absent, skip this file entirely.*

---

## Overview

Building well-characterized intimate dynamics follows the same principles as everything else in character design: behavioral descriptions, friction, and causality produce better results than labels. "Dominant" gives the LLM three stock phrases on loop. A behavioral description of how and why the character takes control — and when they don't — produces dynamic, varied interactions.

The decision about whether to include intimate dynamics — and for which characters — is made once at the project planning stage, recorded in the project plan, and reflected in the roster. It is not revisited character by character.

---

## Relationship and Attraction Dynamics

- How does the character express attraction or interest? Direct or indirect? Obvious flirtation or subtle behavior? Do they test limits, hold back, or move forward?
- What makes them hesitate, pull back, or set a limit? Even in adult scenarios, characters with limits are more interesting than characters without them. What slows them down or draws a line? This creates tension and pacing.

---

## Behavioral Intimacy Descriptions

Use the same When/Behavior/Because formula:

> When [intimate trigger], [character] [intimate behavior], because [reason].

*Example: "When physical closeness escalates, she becomes intensely focused and deliberate — control is how she processes vulnerability, and letting go feels like freefall."*

### The friction in their intimate life

The same contradiction principle from behavioral personality applies here. Someone who craves physical intimacy but panics at emotional intimacy. Someone dominant in public who wants to surrender in private. Someone tender who thinks tenderness is weakness.

Friction in intimate dynamics prevents repetitive output. Without it, the LLM settles into a loop of escalation with no variation. With it, every encounter has texture and the possibility of genuine character development.

---

## Connecting a Dynamic to Character Depth

If the character is built around a specific dynamic, causality and behavioral description outperform labels. A tag gives the LLM a word to cycle through. A character whose dynamic is rooted in their psychology gives the LLM an engine that generates varied, textured scenes.

**The dynamic itself:**
- What is it? Name it plainly.
- What does it give them emotionally that they don't get elsewhere? Every dynamic meets a need. What emotional state does this let the character access? Why can they only access it here?

*Example: A character drawn to restraint — in every other area of their life they are the person holding everything together, the reliable one, the planner. Being physically restrained is the only context where they have permission to stop being in charge. The ropes are not about helplessness. They are about someone else saying "I've got this, you can let go."*

**How it connects to who they are:**
- How does this dynamic relate to their psychology from the character foundation? Does it reinforce something from their history? Contradict it? Compensate for it?
- How does their behavior inside this dynamic differ from their behavior outside it? The contrast between modes tells the LLM something important.

**Edges and specificity:**
- What is adjacent to their dynamic that they would refuse, and why? Limits reveal character as much as desires do.
- What could a partner do within the dynamic that would hit a nerve or make them shut down?

## Behavioral Descriptions for the Dynamic

Write 1–3 behavioral descriptions using the When/Behavior/Because formula.

*Example 1: "When a partner shows genuine submission, she becomes almost tender — protective rather than punishing — because what she actually wants is not control over someone but proof that someone trusts her enough to be defenseless."*

*Example 2: "When an encounter starts to feel scripted or performative, he pulls back and gets visibly irritated. He needs it to be real. If someone is acting out a role to please him rather than because they want it, the whole thing collapses for him."*
